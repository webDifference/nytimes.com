11 Great Titles Expiring From Netflix in December

By Jason Bailey
From left: “It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia,” “Young Frankenstein” and “Practical Magic.”FX; 20th Century Fox; Warner Bros.

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation website. Sign up for our thrice-weekly newsletter here.

Every month, as various licenses expire, streaming services lose movies and TV series from their catalogs. Here are 11 great movies and TV shows leaving Netflix in December.

Gene Wilder and, on the table as the monster, Peter Boyle in “Young Frankenstein.”20th Century Fox, via Getty Images

‘Young Frankenstein’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 1

Arguably Mel Brooks’s finest and funniest film (and released, shockingly enough, in the same calendar year as “Blazing Saddles,” his other contender for that title), this 1974 gem finds the master of broad satire taking on the Universal horror pictures of the 1930s, particularly James Whale’s “Frankenstein” and “Bride of Frankenstein.” Co-writer Gene Wilder is sublimely manic as Dr. Frederick Frankenstein — pronounced “Fronk-en-steen,” he insists — grandson of the famed corpse re-animator Victor, who returns to his grandfather’s estate and laboratory to claim his inheritance and finds himself drawn into the family business. Brooks and Wilder’s Oscar-nominated script is a hit parade of comedy classics, from the candlestick bit to “Puttin’ on the Ritz” to the monster’s dinner with a blind hermit (an unlisted Gene Hackman). But most surprising is its narrative discipline (relative to the rest of the Brooks filmography), which generates genuine pathos and charm.
—

Morgan Spurlock in ”Super Size Me.”Avi Gerver/Roadside Attractions — Samuel Goldwyn Films

‘Super Size Me’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 1

This ingenious (and often uproarious) Oscar-nominated documentary from Morgan Spurlock begins with a clever gimmick: If you ate nothing but McDonald’s for thirty days, how much damage would you do to your body? Quite a lot, it turns out. Spurlock is not only the project’s director but its guinea pig, subjecting himself to a month’s worth of Big Macs, Egg McMuffins, and supersized fries while using the experiment as an opportunity to explore the dominance of the fast-food industry, the deceptiveness of its marketing and the shifting goal posts of public health.
—

Animated versions of Julie Delpy and Ethan Hawke in “Waking Life.”Fox Searchlight Pictures

‘Waking Life’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 1

After scoring with the more conventional narratives of “Dazed and Confused” and “Before Sunrise,” the writer and director Richard Linklater returned to the wandering, free-form style of his debut film, “Slacker,” with this experimental 2001 drama, which fuses elements of fiction, documentary, philosophy and animation to create a playfully surrealistic dreamscape. Along the way, our protagonist (“Dazed” star Wiley Wiggins) encounters everyone from Linklater to Steven Soderbergh to Alex Jones to Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy (reprising their “Before Sunrise” characters). It’s a slippery and stimulating piece of work, full of brainy and bizarre discussions of existentialism, reality, politics, art and dreaming itself.
—

Donal Logue, foreground, and Michael Raymond-James in “Terriers.”Patrick McElhenney/FX

‘Terriers’ Season 1
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 1

This “one-season wonder” from creator Ted Griffin (who wrote the “Ocean’s Eleven” remake) concerns a busted-out ex-cop and a former criminal, two pals who team up to become private detectives. But this is no mere procedural. Griffin and his writers are less interested in the crimes than in the complicated characters who are investigating (and committing) them, crafting a series that leaves the viewer perpetually unbalanced; you’re never quite sure if you’re going to get a breezy chase scene or a raw, emotional verbal duet. It’s only 13 episodes, so it’s a quick binge. It’s also a sad one, since these characters clearly had so many more stories to tell.
—

Sandra Bullock in “Practical Magic.”Warner Bros.

‘Practical Magic’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 1

Sandra Bullock and Nicole Kidman star in this 1998 dark comedy with a romantic streak. The marquee stars create a believable family dynamic as two small-town witch sisters battling not only the prejudices of their neighbors but also a long-lasting love curse: Bullock underplays nicely as a woman attempting to hide her supernatural leanings while Kidman entertainingly embraces her character’s boisterous, unapologetic nature. Goran Visnjic and Aidan Quinn smolder as the sisters’ love interests; Stockard Channing and Dianne Wiest are a hoot as their eccentric aunts.
—

Khleo Thomas, left, and Shia LaBeouf in “Holes.”Phil Bray/Walt Disney Pictures

‘Holes’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 5

Louis Sachar’s beloved young adult novel “Holes” gets the Disney treatment with this 2003 film adaptation, in which the Texas teen of an unlucky family is shipped off to a strange juvenile correction camp, where he is expected to dig holes all day for reasons unclear. Shia LaBeouf shines in his breakthrough role as the central teen; Sigourney Weaver, Jon Voight, Patricia Arquette and Tim Blake Nelson add levity and a considerable amount of weirdness as the grown-ups (in age, if not maturity). The film is eccentric and affable — and engagingly orchestrated by director Andrew Davis (best known for action epics like “The Fugitive” and “Under Siege”).
—

Clockwise from left: Charlie Day, Rob McElhenney, Glenn Howerton, Kaitlin Olson and Danny DeVito in “It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia.”Patrick McElhenney/FX

‘It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia’ Seasons 1-11
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 9

The time has come to say goodbye (at least temporarily) to a Netflix standby, as this long-running FX comedy of oblivious self-absorption takes its leave of the streaming service. Set primarily at Paddy’s Pub in South Philadelphia, the series concerns five borderline sociopaths who test the limits of one another’s (and society’s) tolerance on a daily basis; it’s like “Seinfeld,” but with the egocentricity and narcissism cranked up to 11. And that’s part of the appeal: Its characters’ gleeful willingness to unapologetically embrace their worst natures gives the show a strange freedom from both narrative and moral norms, and the indie-movie vibe of its aesthetics makes it feel far more dangerous than your average sitcom.
—

Jake Gyllenhaal in “Nightcrawler.”Chuck Zlotnick/Open Road Films

‘Nightcrawler’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 10

Jake Gyllenhaal is unnerving and absorbing as a modern-day “Taxi Driver” in this frequently troubling tale of an unstable misfit’s rapid ascension to the top of television news. Slithering through the Los Angeles nightscape, armed with an HD video camera and a questionable sense of ethics, Gyllenhaal’s Louis Bloom will go anywhere for the story — and if he can’t find one, he’ll engineer one. The writer and director Dan Gilroy immerses the viewer in this sticky subset of crime journalism and bracingly dramatizes the ease with which this literal ambulance chaser traverses the bridge into mainstream media. Anchoring these increasingly relevant questions is a gripping performance by Gyllenhaal, who stares this savvy climber right in the eyes and glimpses the horrifying hollowness behind them.
—

Jackie Siegel, center, in “The Queen of Versailles.”Lauren Greenfield/Magnolia Pictures

‘The Queen of Versailles’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 13

Perhaps the most trenchant (and entertaining) commentary on the 2008 financial crisis, this biting documentary concerns David and Jackie Siegel, an obscenely rich Florida couple who are in the process of building a comically outsized mansion when the real estate bubble bursts. Director Lauren Greenfield squeezes the expected gasp-laughter regarding the couple’s insane consumption and their subsequent attempts to curb it. But she isn’t content to take cheap shots. She sees her subjects as both perpetrators and victims of a system of conspicuous capitalism, in which too much is somehow never enough.
—

From left: Demian Bichir as Fidel Castro and Benicio Del Toro as Che in “Che.”Laura Magruder/IFC Films

‘Che’ Parts 1 and 2
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 20

Director Steven Soderbergh’s two-part biopic of the revolutionary leader Che Guevara (Benicio Del Toro) runs a total of four and a half hours, yet its scope is surprisingly small, with each part focusing on a key moment in its subject’s life and letting those snapshots tell his story. The approach creates an uncommonly intimate portrait: Rather than the dramatized Wikipedia page of too many cradle-to-grave biographical films, Soderbergh’s films put the viewer on Guevara’s elbow as he engineers the successful Cuban revolution in 1956 and fumbles a failed uprising in Bolivia nine years later. Soderbergh’s direction is precise and pithy (as usual), and Del Toro is electrifying in the title role.
—

Gael García Bernal in “Amores Perros.”Lions Gate Films

‘Amores Perros’
Leaving Netflix: Dec. 24

This Mexican thriller was an international breakthrough for director Alejandro G. Iñárritu; screenwriter Guillermo Arriaga; and its star, Gael García Bernal. It tells three stories, seemingly unconnected, which literally collide at the scene of a Mexico City automobile accident, but this is no mere exercise in narrative gimmickry. Iñárritu and Arriaga use the intersections and divergences of their stories to paint a vivid picture of urban life, and of the seedy underbelly that propels it.
—
