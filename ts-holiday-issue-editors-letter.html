    T’s Holiday Issue: Editor’s Letter

    By HANYA YANAGIHARANOV. 29, 2017

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Anyone who’s ever read a profile, edited a profile or been the subject of a profile knows that the difference between a revelatory interview and a banal one depends almost as much — if not more so — on the interlocutor as on the subject. The interviewee — no matter how eloquent, insightful and generous he may be in life — is, in the moment he sits down, a cipher; the concern is that he will either say nothing at all, or, worse, say nothing of meaning: that he will become a persona, and that instead of conversation, what follows will be a kind of performance, a recitation of what he knows we have come to expect. A good interviewer, however, is able to transcend the inherent artificiality of the moment, to make the encounter feel less like testimony and more like a real dialogue (albeit one heavily weighted toward only one participant’s thoughts and opinions).

    There were many reasons why we were honored to have The New York Times’s executive editor, Dean Baquet, interview our cover subject, Jay-Z, but a primary one was Baquet’s superior talents as a reporter. As someone who is not a reporter herself but who has spent much of her career working with and admiring them, I am always in awe of how naturally, and with what grace, the best of them can talk to, truly, anyone, and the fluid, rhythmic game of conversational catch they can encourage. Great reporters begin their inquiries with genuine curiosity and real humility — usually, their hope is not to have proven what they suspect or want to hear, but to learn something new, something you can only learn from talking to somebody else … which is, after all, the point of journalism, and of the newsroom that Baquet leads.

    Beyond Baquet’s skills as a journalist, however, there was another thing that made this pairing exceptional. Here are two black men, 13 years apart in age, each dominant in his respective field, each powerful in realms — media; business (Jay-Z is the founder of the record label Roc Nation and co-owner of the music-streaming company Tidal) — whose pinnacles are still dominated by white men. Their conversation was, therefore, as much an interview as it was an exchange between peers, and among the topics they discussed were some that were deeply personal: What does it mean to raise black children in America in 2017, and in very different circumstances than they themselves were raised in? As successful black men, do they owe anything to their community (and what is that community, and what is that definition of success)? The result is a discussion as intimate as it is wide-ranging, one that allows us the privilege of seeing — or, rather, re-seeing — one of the world’s most inventive, brilliant and influential rappers grapple with fatherhood, marriage and the most enduring and universal question of all: what it means to be a good person.

    That last concern is one every thinking person should and must wonder about, and this has been a year in which many of us (and for many reasons) have been forced to consider it with more depth and frequency than we’d perhaps like to. And yet we must. Governments and politicians come and go, but the ability to practice and demonstrate personal decency, to say and do the things that make life fairer and more just for the weakest and least privileged among us, is inalienable, and the truest exertion of personal autonomy. It is something to celebrate: at holiday time, of course, but always, and is therefore the most profound gift of all.
