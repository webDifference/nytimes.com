    ‘Elmet’ Backs a Brawny Man Into a Dangerous Corner

    Books of The Times

    By JOHN WILLIAMS NOV. 27, 2017

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    The poet Ted Hughes once wrote about the landscape of North England near where he was born: “Death-struggle of the glacier/Enlarged the long gullet of Calder/Down which its corpse vanished.”

    There’s a lot of death-struggle — and a corpse or two or a few — in 29-year-old Fiona Mozley’s debut novel, a lyrical and mythic work that was shortlisted for this year’s Man Booker Prize. Mozley grew up in the York area, like Hughes, and her novel is set in the Yorkshire countryside, which long ago was the Celtic kingdom of Elmet.

    “Elmet” derives a good deal of its power from the dichotomy between its primary character — a taciturn giant named John who makes his living as an undefeated bare-knuckle brawler in underground fights — and its narrator, John’s sensitive 14-year-old son, Daniel.

    Daniel and his tougher, more resourceful sister, 15-year-old Cathy, live with their father in a remote wood in Northern England, in a home John built himself. This move was most directly prompted by Cathy being reprimanded at school for fighting back against bullies. “He wanted to keep us separate, in ourselves, apart from the world,” Daniel says, and so the siblings are raised in this “strange, sylvan otherworld” where they make their own bows and arrows. Cathy rolls her own cigarettes. They eat doves, deer and skylarks.

    They do all this rugged living on a land “made of myths,” as Daniel describes it. Mozley is working toward a Ph.D. in medieval history, and her story is rooted, actually and tonally, in ancient soil. The mentions, early on, of cars and television sets are surprising, some of the first indicators that we’re anywhere north of, say, the 12th century. The successful execution of this bold strategy, to voice a story set in the present day as if it could be happening nearly any time in human history, is just one indicator of Mozley’s skill and ambition.

    There is little work to be had for the novel’s characters; the jobs left the region “20 years ago or more.” Now farm owners take advantage of seasonal workers, and locals feel increasingly alienated. “I’m paying to live on a piece of land,” one woman complains, “that we, all of us, used to own together.”

    John has built his family’s home on land owned by a man named Price, who controls much of the surrounding area. He and John, it turns out, have a fraught history. In an attempt to hurt Price, John helps several workers and tenants organize to fight for better wages and lower rents. Steinbeckian themes of collectivism move to the fore. Whether Price will let John peaceably live on the land he has claimed for himself becomes the novel’s considerable dramatic fuel.

    Where is the children’s mother? “We almost never spoke of her,” Daniel says, and John’s “mentioning her was so rare that we did not know whether to take it as an invitation or as a warning.”

    John sends the children to spend days with a nearby family friend named Vivien, a normalizing and educative influence. Vivien is worldly, and teaches Daniel about art and travel while Cathy runs through the fields and woods. (“I had an inside sort of head. She had an outside sort of head,” Daniel says of him and his sister.) Vivien also teaches Daniel how to think of his father’s tendencies, comparing John’s need for violence to a whale’s occasional breaching in one of Mozley’s many arresting passages about the natural world.

    Daniel, pale and skinny with long hair, is in many ways the opposite of his father and his sister, whose strength is badly misjudged by those who provoke her. “You have to appreciate that I never thought of myself as a man,” he explains at one point in a moving and understated passage about his still-forming gender identification.

    Mozley’s language is often subtle, but her protagonist is not. Physically, John might as well be Jack Reacher, the invincible hulk who wanders through Lee Child’s best-selling series of thrillers. Daniel describes his father as “gargantuan,” a foot taller than even the most intimidating men in the area; “each of his arms was as thick as two of theirs.” Philosophically, he wouldn’t be out of place in an Ayn Rand novel, given the cult he makes out of self-sufficiency. “I won’t work for any man ever again,” he says. “My body is my own. It is all I own.” (In believing that all he needs is to be left alone, John neatly shows where Rand’s Supermen overlap with the frontier mentality of Laura Ingalls Wilder’s less pretentious heroes; where modesty meets megalomania.)

    Debut novels especially can sometimes too clearly betray their debts, but “Elmet” is a beguiling patchwork of influences held together by Mozley’s distinct voice. It begins with an italicized, hazy remembrance of a fiery scene, ending with: “The remains of Elmet lie beneath my feet.” If it recalls Hughes (“Remains of Elmet” being the title of the poem quoted above), it also carries a whisper of the way Manderley is remembered at the beginning of Daphne du Maurier’s gothic classic “Rebecca.” Mozley has cited Cormac McCarthy’s “Blood Meridian” as a favorite novel, and though she doesn’t try to sustain McCarthy’s sonorous tone — a disastrous temptation to which some writers succumb — she occasionally channels his nature-besotted intensity: “The dawn erupted from a bud of mauve half-light and bloomed bloody as I woke.”

    In its signposting and pacing, “Elmet” promises a reckoning, and we get one. The climactic scene is full of bedlam. It is also cartoonish. One might balk at its outlandishness, or squirm at its vivid, protracted violence, but it keeps your attention and doesn’t leave any fireworks unpopped.

    Mozley’s characters sometimes say things they would never say; Price’s villainy is too pure; the story’s political complexities about property ownership and labor are too efficiently reduced. But despite the book’s frequent attention to realistic details, it is securely situated in fable territory, and Mozley’s sheer storytelling confidence sends the reader sailing past almost every speed bump.

    Elmet
    By Fiona Mozley
    312 pages. Algonquin. $15.95.
