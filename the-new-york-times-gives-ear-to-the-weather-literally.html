    It’s Been a Constant for 120 Years. Yet It Changes With the Weather.

    By STEPHEN HILTNERNOV. 27, 2017

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    You might say that The New York Times has an ear for the weather.

    For more than 120 years — since Feb. 10, 1897 — The New York Times has printed a weather forecast on the top right-hand corner of the front page: its right “ear,” in newspaper parlance. (Our motto, “All the News That’s Fit to Print,” has run opposite it, on the left ear, since the very same day.)

    It’s tempting to think of the weather ear as the simplest element of The Times’s most prestigious page. In fact, it’s one of the front page’s most dynamic features: Aside from changes that might emerge between early and late editions of the paper, the weather ear is the only part of the page that differs from region to region.

    All told, 27 different weather ears appear each day — one for each location where The New York Times is printed. (Our main printing plant in College Point, Queens, prints and distributes papers in a roughly 100-mile radius around New York City. But The Times also makes use of 26 other print facilities around the country. Each serves a particular region, and each gets its own forecast.)

    Newsroom tradition dictates that the responsibility for compiling each day’s weather ears falls to the night news assistant on the international desk. The initial copy comes directly from AccuWeather; it’s largely rewritten to fit into the fixed five-line box of text — and to jibe with New York Times style.

    “It’s kind of like writing a haiku,” said Remy Tumin, a news assistant who has written nearly a thousand weather ears during her two years with The Times. “We also get to play God, to a degree,” she added with a laugh. “If ‘a mix of sun and clouds’ doesn’t fit on the page, we get to say ‘partly cloudy.’”

    When The Times’s weather ear first appeared, forecasts were a relatively new phenomenon. The formation of the National Weather Service, in 1870, laid the foundations for regular weather coverage, and within a few years The Times was reporting daily on the previous day’s weather — and providing a forecast for parts of the Northeast. Finally, in 1897, the forecast made its leap to the front page. (The New York World, it should be noted — a Times competitor then run by Joseph Pulitzer — was the first to pioneer a weather ear on its front page.)

    What filled our ears from 1851 to 1897? Mostly nothing. The nameplate was often given a sort of lonely prominence — although several other designs were tried and eventually discarded. For a few of The Times’s earliest years, when the paper was called The New-York Daily Times, the left ear announced our publication schedule and subscription rates; the right ear simply contained the news, in a column that was taller than the others. And there was a stretch of time when the ears were used to announce the number of pages in a given day’s paper.

    Of course, now most readers can consult mobile weather apps to find a local forecast. But The Times’s weather ear is here to stay. “We’ve never really seriously considered changing it,” said Tom Bodkin, who, as The Times’s creative director, designs each day’s front page. “It’s part of our tradition, and people do actually use it as a quick way to find the weather in the morning.”

    Put another way: The weather ear won’t be out on its ear anytime soon.

    And that isn’t exactly music to the international news assistants’ ears, since compiling the weather ears can sometimes be a tricky task — especially on busy shifts when they are already up to their ears in the news.

    “The other night, I was working on the ears,” Ms. Tumin said, “but there was also a potential coup happening in Zimbabwe. So I was making calls all over the world — but I was also worried about whether it was going to be sunny in Seattle.”
