    How to Do Broadway

    NOV. 21, 2017

    What to see, where to eat and how to get cheap tickets – everything you need to navigate the holiday season.

    How to Do Broadway

    What to See Getting Tickets Where to Eat At Showtime

    What to See

    The sheer number of productions can be overwhelming, so this isn’t a comprehensive list. It’s a sampler of shows of particular interest, including a few holiday-themed offerings outside Times Square. (A complete set of theater reviews by Times critics is here.)

    Long-running Crowd Pleasers

    The Book of Mormon

    This gleefully profane musical comedy about a pair of Mormon missionaries seeking converts in Uganda is not for the prim. But if you’ve loved other irreverent hits by the team that hatched this one – Trey Parker and Matt Stone (“South Park”) and Robert Lopez (“Avenue Q”) – it’s an excellent bet.

    Kinky Boots

    Billy Porter, who won a 2013 Tony Award for his scene-stealing performance as Lola, the drag artist at the center of this Cyndi Lauper-Harvey Fierstein musical, has returned to the production through the holidays, along with his original co-star, Stark Sands. Go for Ms. Lauper’s pop score, and to be wowed by Mr. Porter in those sparkly, ruby-red, thigh-high stilettos.

    The Phantom of the Opera

    Ronald Reagan was in the White House when this Andrew Lloyd Webber gothic romance opened on Broadway and became a gargantuan hit. The masked phantom has been haunting the Paris Opera House, smitten with his Christine, ever since. If you’re a Lloyd Webber devotee, see it if the touring production isn’t coming soon to a theater near you – though chances are it is.

    Wicked

    Kristin Chenoweth and Idina Menzel bowed out long ago as Glinda and her nemesis, Elphaba, but Stephen Schwartz and Winnie Holzman’s musical spin on “The Wizard of Oz” has remained a strong draw for 14 years. It’s the smash that woke Broadway producers up to the voraciousness of girls as a demographic. For teens, tweens and “Oz” fans of all ages.

    Critics’ Darlings

    The Band's Visit

    Tony Shalhoub and Katrina Lenk star in David Yazbek and Itamar Moses’s musical about a bumbling Egyptian police band’s arrival in an Israeli desert town. An Off Broadway hit last season, it’s directed by David Cromer and based on the charming film of the same name. Poignant and bittersweet, this is a gentle comic romance, free of politics and full of gorgeous music.

    Come From Away

    There are no stars in this bighearted musical about the generosity that greeted a group of airline passengers stranded in Newfoundland in the days after the 9/11 attacks. A feel-good show based on a true story, it taps into a nostalgia for that not-so-distant time when this country and much of the rest of the world got along.

    Dear Evan Hansen

    What will this production, which won the 2017 Tony for best musical, be without Ben Platt, who got a Tony of his own for his tear-drenched turn as the troubled teenage Evan? Audiences are about to find out, starting Nov. 21 with Noah Galvin (of the ABC sitcom “The Real O’Neals”) in the role, then in mid-January with Taylor Trensch, fresh from playing Barnaby Tucker in “Hello, Dolly!” For Mr. Platt’s many worshipful fans, his performance will be hard to match. Happily, Rachel Bay Jones, a fellow Tony winner, is sticking around as Evan’s mom.

    Farinelli and the King

    The three-time Tony winner Mark Rylance returns to the intimate Belasco Theater as King Philippe V of Spain, whose roiled mind is soothed by the singing of a castrato named Farinelli. Claire van Kampen, who directed Mr. Rylance, her husband, in “Nice Fish” last year Off Broadway, is the playwright and composer. Starting previews Dec. 5, the show earned six Olivier Award nominations for its London run.

    For Kids and Their Grownups

    Anastasia

    The best way to see this Russian-princess musical is with a crowd of tween girls, whose rapturous response to its romance – like their devotion to the animated film that partly inspired the show – heightens the fun. Adults can also savor the wondrous Mary Beth Peil as the Dowager Empress.

    Charlie and the Chocolate Factory

    For a musical with a song called “Pure Imagination,” this adaptation of the Roald Dahl children’s book comes up short in that department. Starring Christian Borle as Willy Wonka, the show does boast the incomparable Jackie Hoffman as Mrs. Teavee. Her shoes are fabulous. (Through Jan. 14)

    The Lion King

    Lush with masks and puppetry, Julie Taymor’s visually extravagant retelling of the Disney animated classic is that rare beast: a high-art spectacle that’s also an enduring commercial blockbuster. With a score by Elton John and Tim Rice, additional music by the South African composer Lebo M., and choreography by Garth Fagan, this musical just turned 20.

    SpongeBob SquarePants

    Bikini Bottom fans are the natural audience of this musical spinoff of the Nickelodeon cartoon. In previews for a Dec. 4 opening, it aims for the sweet spot with colorful, kid-friendly design and songs by grownup favorites like Sara Bareilles, the Flaming Lips, Panic! At the Disco, David Bowie and more.

    Holiday Fare (on Broadway and Off)

    Radio City Christmas Spectacular

    The Rockettes in all their precision kick-line glory, the art deco splendor of Radio City Music Hall – even the teeming throngs on the sidewalk outside are part of the tradition. The holiday trappings are fun, but the Rockettes’ breathtaking athleticism is the real draw. (Through Jan. 1)

    The Dead, 1904

    Melissa Gilbert inherits the role Kate Burton had last year in Paul Muldoon and Jean Hanff Korelitz’s adaptation of James Joyce’s short story. Performed in a Fifth Avenue townhouse, this is immersive theater in the guise of an elegant party – with wine, spirits and a sit-down dinner. (Through Jan. 7)

    Elf: The Musical

    George Wendt, who played Santa Claus in the original Broadway cast, reprises the part at the cavernous Theater at Madison Square Garden. Adapted from the Will Ferrell comedy about a human boy raised as one of Santa’s more inept helpers. (Dec. 13-29)

    Home for the Holidays

    Three reality-T.V. singing champs, one Broadway concert. Candice Glover (“American Idol”), Josh Kaufman (“The Voice”) and Bianca Ryan (“America’s Got Talent”) star; Kaitlyn Bristowe of “The Bachelorette” hosts. Curveball alert: The actor Danny Aiello also makes an appearance. (Through Dec. 30)

    (Nearly) Impossible Tickets

    Hamilton

    It’s not that there’s no chance of buying your way in to see Lin-Manuel Miranda’s game-changing musical-theater phenomenon. But when the Ticketmaster website asks, “What’s your budget per ticket?,” then suggests a range from, say, $500 to $1,400, it may give you pause. The show has a digital lottery for $10 orchestra seats, though — 46 at every performance. Slim odds, but people win them every day.

    Springsteen on Broadway

    There was surely no more surprising Broadway debut this fall than Mr. Springsteen’s ecstatically received performance in this up-close, almost-solo show (his wife, Patti Scialfa, provides occasional harmony). The limited run is, of course, sold out, aside from a digital lottery that dangles the chance of $75 tickets. There are 26 for each show. So maybe?

    Hello, Dolly!

    On Tuesday nights, when the acclaimed Donna Murphy steps into the title role, tickets become possible. But the rest of the week, astronomical sums are required to see Bette Midler’s Dolly. When Ms. Midler exits the show in January, her Horace Vandergelder (David Hyde Pierce) leaves, too.

    How to Get Tickets

    Let’s get this out of the way: If it’s the toast of Broadway, don’t expect to get a deal. The shows offering discounts may still be in previews (which means critics haven’t yet weighed in) or have been around a while and are running low on fuel. Still, some excellent productions might be in the mix.

    Same-Day Strategies

    TKTS

    TKTS, that discount-ticket mainstay of Times Square, also has outlets at Lincoln Center, at South Street Seaport and in downtown Brooklyn. The Times Square booth has the longest hours, but it’s the only location that sells same-day matinee tickets. (The other locations sell same-day evening but only next-day matinee tickets.) On the TKTS app, or online at tdf.org, you can see in real time which shows are on sale, and for how great a discount at each location. But that doesn’t mean there will be any seats left for the show you want by the time you get up to the window, and you have to buy them in person. The Times Square booth is the most crowded, especially right after it opens, when options are most plentiful. But new tickets are released all day, even as curtain time nears, so going later can be lucky, too. Want to see a play rather than a musical? At Times Square, there’s a dedicated window for that, and the line is shorter.

    Rush Tickets

    Many shows, though not the monster hits, offer same-day rush tickets at the box office for much less than full price. Some – including “Hello, Dolly!,” “Dear Evan Hansen” and “The Book of Mormon” – sell standing-room tickets if a show is sold out. Don’t count on these approaches, because availability varies – but it’s worth swinging by the theater to check. Conveniently, Playbill keeps a running online tab of individual shows’ policies on lotteries, rush tickets (sometimes just for students, often for everyone), standing room and other discounts.

    In-Person Lotteries

    Some shows (lately including “The Book of Mormon” and “Wicked”) have in-person lotteries, where you go to the theater at a designated time on the day of the performance and put your name into a drawing for the chance to buy cheap tickets. It’s more work on your end than a digital lottery, but these tickets can be substantially less expensive than those.

    Box Office

    Getting your tickets at the box office has its advantages – including that you don’t have to pay the hefty service fees that are tacked onto online orders. And if you have a discount code, like the ones offered on theatermania.com, it should work in person, too.

    (A Note on Bad Weather)

    Is rain or snow cascading from the heavens? That may be your chance to snap up some suddenly available seats at the box office, though be prepared to pay face value. Your odds of winning a ticket lottery are better on days like that, too.

    Buying Ahead

    TodayTix App

    The TodayTix app is a trustworthy source for discounted Broadway tickets, which users buy online as much as 30 days in advance, then pick up from a uniformed TodayTix employee at showtime outside the theater. But you don’t know where your seats are until you pick them up. The app can also be used for entering some shows’ digital lotteries, which offer the chance to buy cheap tickets.

    Box Office or Show Site

    To guard against heartbreak, the safest bet is to buy through the show’s website or at the box office. If you’re prepared to shell out for an experience like “Hamilton,” Ticketmaster offers verified resale tickets, and the musical’s own website will send you there. But there are counterfeits floating in cyberspace, too, so beware of offers that seem too good to be true.

    Ticket Services

    Services like Play-by-Play and Theatermania’s Gold Club, which offer members very inexpensive tickets that have gone unsold, are more useful for Off Broadway shows. You might find a Broadway show listed, but that’s less likely at this high-demand time of year.

    Where to Eat?

    If you’re interested not so much in dining as in grabbing a quick and unmemorable meal, Times Square is perfect. If you’re looking for a nice dinner, that’s a trickier proposition – a perennial quandary even for Broadway regulars. We’ve provided some nearby recommendations from the Times restaurant critic Pete Wells.

    Plan Your Night

    Eat Late

    Evening curtain times are usually around 8 p.m., but double-check your tickets, because it varies. This means you will either be eating absurdly early or waiting until after the performance. If you can hold off (maybe getting a pre-show slice of pizza or a snack at the theater), you’ll have a more leisurely experience – and it’ll give you a chance to talk about the show.

    Make a Reservation

    If possible, make a reservation. Check the show’s running time to calculate when it gets out, then add 15 minutes, because running time doesn’t always include intermission.

    Pete Wells Recommends:

    Benoit

    As close to a true Parisian bistro as you’ll find in Manhattan, Benoit is especially strong on classics like onion soup, quenelles de brochet, cassoulet, soufflés and mille feuilles. It’s possible to make a meal out of the inexpensive hors d’oeuvres, which you tick off with a golf pencil on a small paper menu. 60 West 55th Street (Sixth Avenue); 646-943-7373; benoitny.com.

    Gabriel Kreuther

    Two restaurants in one: a formal dining room for elaborate French-derived dinners and a lounge for more casual cooking, especially Alsatian classics like tarte flambée and country sausage with sauerkraut. 41 West 42nd Street (Sixth Avenue); 212-257-5826; gknyc.com.

    Hakkasan

    It’s worth enduring the Orientalist nightclub atmosphere and mildly preposterous prices for high-style Cantonese cuisine that is often superb, particularly the dumplings and other dim sum dishes. 311 West 43rd Street (Eighth Avenue); 776-1818; hakkasan.com.

    Sen Sakana

    This new restaurant offers a rare opportunity to taste Peru’s version of Japanese food (or maybe it’s Japan’s version of Peruvian food): ceviches and tiraditos, skewered meats and vegetables grilled over charcoal, and sushi that ranges from simple to wildly inventive. 28 West 44th Street (Fifth Avenue); 212-221-9560; sensakana.com.

    Margon

    Wedge yourself in, grab an orange plastic tray and stand in front of the steam tables pointing at Cuban and Dominican cooking: oxtail stew, plaintains, octopus salad with green olives. It’s far from fancy, but it’s one of the neighborhood’s true bargains. 136 West 46th Street (Seventh Avenue); 212-354-5013.

    Sake Bar Hagi

    No reservations means there may be a wait for a table but service is speedy, and the Japanese izakaya food is reliably solid; keep an eye out for the specials posted on the wall. 152 West 49th Street (Seventh Avenue); 212-764-8549; sakebarhagi.com.

    Showtime Strategy

    So you’ve got the tickets and you’re eager to see the curtain rise. Here’s what to expect now that it’s showtime.

    Getting There

    When to Arrive

    Many ticket holders wait in enormous lines snaking down the sidewalks to get in. (There’s a separate, shorter line for the box office.) But you do not have to arrive early to queue up. If you don’t need to pick up your tickets, it’s perfectly fine, and more convenient, to show up five minutes before curtain. Budget extra time if you want to stop in the restroom, where the lines, for women, can be long.

    Driving In

    Save yourself the headache and reserve a parking spot through one of a number of apps, such as BestParking, ParkWhiz and Parking Panda. Lincoln Center also offers its own reserved parking online. Still, allot more driving time than you think you’ll need, especially during the holidays. Not every show admits tardy arrivals. When they do, latecomers risk taking a walk of shame with an usher – and squeezing into their row in the dark.

    Navigating Times Square

    The square itself is a bright cacophony of sensory stimulation and a jostling yet torpid mass of humanity. If you can avoid it, do. If you must walk through it, single file is the way to go. Elsewhere, at the edge of the theater district, foot traffic on the west side of Eighth Avenue moves faster than on the crowd-clogged east side. Likewise, walking north or south on Sixth Avenue, then west to your theater, can be faster.

    What to Wear

    Seats can be tight. Dress comfortably. Jeans are fine; New Yorkers often dress down at the theater. (But if you want to break out your special-occasion attire, that’s O.K., too.) Bring a sweater; it often gets cold. Wearing a hat? Be kind to the people behind you. Take it off inside.

    Special Accommodations

    A laudably comprehensive, easy-to-navigate website, theatreaccess.nyc, can tell you all you need to know – theater by theater, show by show – about wheelchair access (starting at the curb), autism-friendliness and accommodations for people with special visual and auditory needs.

    At the Show and After

    Plan Ahead

    In most Broadway theaters, the women’s restrooms are notoriously small, and the lines can be dauntingly long at just about every one. If possible, use the bathroom elsewhere before you arrive or after you leave. Otherwise, budget extra time, and know that an intermission visit is unlikely to be quick.

    Quiet Down

    Once the lights go down, save your chatter for intermission or afterward. As for cell phones, using them during a performance is actually illegal in New York City. So turn off your devices and put them away. If you don’t, the ushers may track you down and (mortifyingly) make you.

    Get an Autograph

    Not always clearly marked, stage doors are generally off to the side or around back of the theater entrance; barricades and security guards are telltale signs, but ask an usher if it’s not apparent. Some actors love to meet fans, others are more private, and sometimes they simply don’t have time to stop – not even if you’ve been waiting there an hour. Bring your program for an autograph or your phone for a selfie, but keep in mind that no one owes you either. The performance you just saw was what you bought.

    Check Out the Scene

    If you’ve ever wondered what it’s like to get an aftershow drink at a storied Broadway hangout like Sardi’s or Joe Allen – this is the time. These restaurants often attract performers; be respectful, but don’t be shy.
